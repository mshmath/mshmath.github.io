Anatomy of an Exponent

xa

Base (x)

The number being multiplied repeatedly.

Exponent (a)

Tells you how many times to multiply the base.

Rule 1: Product Rule

Multiplying Powers? ADD Exponents!

If the bases are the same, keep the base and add the small numbers.

xa ⋅ xb = xa+b
Why does this work?
x² ⋅ x³
means
(x ⋅ x) ⋅ (x ⋅ x ⋅ x)

Count them up: That's 5 x's total!

= x⁵

Rule 2: Quotient Rule

Dividing Powers? SUBTRACT Exponents!

If the bases are the same, keep the base and subtract top minus bottom.

xa xb = xa-b
Why does this work?
x⁵ x²
means
x ⋅ x ⋅ x ⋅ x ⋅ x x ⋅ x

2 pairs cancel out. How many are left on top?

= x³

Rule 3: Power Rule

Power to a Power? MULTIPLY!

When a power is raised to another power (parentheses), multiply them.

(xa)b = xa⋅b
Why does this work?
(x²)³
means 3 groups of (x²)
(x ⋅ x) ⋅ (x ⋅ x) ⋅ (x ⋅ x)

Count them up: That's 6 x's total!

= x⁶

Advanced Concepts: Zero & Negative

Zero Exponent

Anything to the power of zero is 1.

x⁰ = 1

(Exceptions: 0⁰ is undefined)

Negative Exponents

Negative power? Flip the fraction.

x⁻ᵃ = 1xᵃ

"Elevator Rule": Move it down to make it positive.

Guided Practice: Zero & Negative

1. 7x⁰
7(1) = 7
2. (3y)⁰
Whole group = 1
3. x⁻⁴
Flip it = 1x⁴

Coefficients vs. Exponents

Important Distinction

Treat the big numbers (coefficients) normally! Only the exponents follow the special rules.

Multiply
(2x³)(4x²)

Multiply 2 ⋅ 4 = 8

Add 3 + 2 = 5

8x⁵
Divide
10x⁸2x²

Divide 10 / 2 = 5

Subtract 8 - 2 = 6

5x⁶
