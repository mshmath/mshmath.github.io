Unit 1: Logic & Reasoning

Inductive Reasoning

Using observed patterns or specific examples to make a guess (conjecture) about what comes next.
Example
2, 4, 6, 8... Next is 10

Deductive Reasoning

Using facts, definitions, and logic rules to prove a conclusion must be true.
Example
Proofs & Laws of Logic

Conditional

An "If-Then" statement. It links a hypothesis ($p$) to a conclusion ($q$).
Symbol
$\mathbf{\color{#2563eb}p} \rightarrow \mathbf{\color{#dc2626}q}$

Hypothesis ($p$)

The "If" part of a conditional statement. It is the given information.
Example
If it rains, then...

Conclusion ($q$)

The "Then" part of a conditional statement. It is the result.
Example
...then the grass is wet.

Negation ($\sim$)

The opposite of a statement. We usually just add the word "not".
Symbol
$\sim p$ (Read as "Not p")

Converse

Formed by FLIPPING the order of the hypothesis and conclusion.
Symbol
$\mathbf{\color{#dc2626}q} \rightarrow \mathbf{\color{#2563eb}p}$

Inverse

Formed by NEGATING (adding "not") to both the hypothesis and conclusion.
Symbol
$\sim \mathbf{p} \rightarrow \sim \mathbf{q}$

Contrapositive

Formed by FLIPPING and NEGATING both parts.
Symbol
$\sim \mathbf{q} \rightarrow \sim \mathbf{p}$

Biconditional

A statement that contains the phrase "if and only if". It works forwards and backwards.
Symbol
$p \leftrightarrow q$

Counterexample

A specific example that proves a statement is FALSE.
Example
"All birds fly."
Counterexample: Penguin

Law of Detachment

If the rule "$p \rightarrow q$" is true, and we know $p$ happened, then $q$ must happen.
Conclusion
$\therefore q$ is True

Law of Syllogism

The Chain Rule. If A implies B, and B implies C, then A implies C.
Example
If $A \to B$ and $B \to C$,
then $A \to C$
