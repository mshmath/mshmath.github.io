Unit 1: Number Sense & Rational Numbers

Day 1: Integers on a Number Line & Absolute Value

Step-by-Step Notes (Click to Expand)

1. What is an Integer?
Integers are whole numbers. They are not fractions or decimals.
They include all positive whole numbers, all negative whole numbers, and zero (0).

Visualizing Integers

2. Number Line & Comparison:
Think of a number line like a bank account.
Numbers to the right are bigger (you have more money).
Numbers to the left are smaller (you owe money or have less).
Example: \(-2\) is bigger than \(-10\). It's better to owe $2 than to owe $10.

Symbols for Comparing

  • < means "less than". (Example: \(4 < 9\))
  • = means "equal to". (Example: \(5 = 5\))
  • > means "greater than". (Example: \(8 > 3\))

Tip: Think of the symbol as an alligator's mouth. It always wants to eat the *bigger* number!

3. Absolute Value (Distance from 0):
Absolute value is a number's distance from zero. That's it!
The answer is always positive (or zero) because you can't walk negative steps!
We use the symbol \(| \dots |\). Example: \(|5| = 5\) and \(|-5| = 5\).

Visualizing Absolute Value

Guided Practice

I Do

Problem: Compare \(-7\) and \(-4\) using <, >, or =.

Solution:

  1. I locate both numbers on a number line.
  2. I see that \(-7\) is to the *left* of \(-4\).
  3. Numbers to the left are *less than* numbers to the right. (It's worse to owe $7 than to owe $4).
  4. Therefore, \(-7 < -4\).

We Do

Problem: Find the value of \(|-8| + |2|\).

  1. First, what is the absolute value of \(-8\)? (How far is -8 from 0? It's 8)
  2. Next, what is the absolute value of \(2\)? (How far is 2 from 0? It's 2)
  3. Now, add the results: \(8 + 2 = 10\).

You Do

Problem: Compare \(-10\) and \(|-11|\) using <, >, or =.

\(-10 < 11\) (Because \(|-11|\) is 11, and \(-10\) is less than \(11\))

Independent Practice

(Green) Find the value of \(|-9|\).

(Yellow) Find the value of \(|-5| - |3|\).

(Red) Is the statement \(|-x| = x\) always, sometimes, or never true? Explain.

Sometimes.
It's true if \(x\) is positive or zero (Example: if \(x=5\), then \(|-(5)| = |-5| = 5\). This works.)
It's false if \(x\) is a negative number. (Example: if \(x = -3\), we plug it in: \(|-(-3)|\). The two negatives make a positive: \(|3|\), which is \(3\). But the original \(x\) was \(-3\). So, \(3\) is not equal to \(-3\).)

Day 2: Adding & Subtracting Integers

Step-by-Step Notes (Click to Expand)

Adding Integers (Combining)

Think of positives as "good guys" and negatives as "bad guys".

  • Same Signs: If signs are the same, ADD the numbers and KEEP the sign.
    (Example: \((-5) + (-3) = -8\). 5 bad guys plus 3 bad guys = 8 bad guys.)
  • Different Signs: If signs are different, SUBTRACT the smaller number from the bigger one.
    KEEP the sign of the bigger number (the one farther from zero).
    (Example: \((-9) + 4\). Subtract: \(9 - 4 = 5\). The 9 is bigger and negative, so the answer is \(-5\).)

Visualizing on a Number Line:

  • Adding a positive: Move to the RIGHT. (Example: \(5 + 2 = 7\))
  • Adding a negative: Move to the LEFT. (Example: \(1 + (-3) = -2\))

Subtracting Integers (Finding the Difference)

This is just like adding, but with one or two simple rules first.

  • Rule 1: Subtracting a Positive (like \(8 - 5\) or \(-2 - 3\))
    Subtracting a positive number means you move to the LEFT on the number line.
    Example: \((-2) - 3\). Start at -2 and move 3 steps to the left. You land on \(-5\).
  • Rule 2: Subtracting a Negative (like \(5 - (-3)\))
    A "double negative" becomes a PLUS. Subtracting a negative is the same as adding a positive.
    Example: \(5 - (-3)\) becomes \(5 + 3\), which equals \(8\).
    Example: \((-7) - (-2)\) becomes \((-7) + 2\), which equals \(-5\).

Guided Practice

I Do

Problem: \((-7) + 4\)

  1. The signs are *different*.
  2. I subtract the numbers: \(7 - 4 = 3\).
  3. The number with the bigger absolute value is \(-7\), which is negative.
  4. My answer is \(-3\).

We Do

Problem: \((-5) - (-9)\)

  1. I see subtraction and a negative: \(- (-9)\). This is Rule 2.
  2. The "double negative" becomes a PLUS.
  3. My new problem is \((-5) + 9\).
  4. The signs are *different*. Subtract: \(9 - 5 = 4\).
  5. Keep the sign of the \(9\) (which is positive). The answer is \(4\).

You Do

Problem: \(10 - 12\)

\(-2\) (This is Rule 1. Start at 10, move 12 steps left. You land at -2.)

Day 3: Multiplying & Dividing Integers

Step-by-Step Notes (Click to Expand)

Good news! The rules for multiplying and dividing are the same, and they are simple.

Rule 1: Same Signs = Positive

If the two numbers have the same sign (both positive or both negative), the answer is ALWAYS POSITIVE.

  • \( (5) \times (3) = 15 \)
  • \( (-5) \times (-3) = 15 \)
  • \( (10) \div (2) = 5 \)
  • \( (-10) \div (-2) = 5 \)

Rule 2: Different Signs = Negative

If the two numbers have different signs (one positive, one negative), the answer is ALWAYS NEGATIVE.

  • \( (-6) \times (3) = -18 \)
  • \( (6) \times (-3) = -18 \)
  • \( (20) \div (-4) = -5 \)
  • \( (-20) \div (4) = -5 \)

Guided Practice

I Do

Problem: \((-6) \times (3)\)

  1. I see the signs are *different* (one negative, one positive).
  2. I know the answer must be *negative* (Rule 2).
  3. I multiply the numbers: \(6 \times 3 = 18\).
  4. My answer is \(-18\).

We Do

Problem: \((-20) \div (-4)\)

  1. What are the signs? (They are the *same*.)
  2. What will the sign of the answer be? (It must be *positive* by Rule 1.)
  3. Now divide the numbers: \(20 \div 4 = 5\).
  4. The answer is \(5\).

You Do

Problem: \((-5) \times (-8)\)

\(40\) (Same signs, so the answer is positive.)

Day 4: Rational Numbers & Equivalence

Step-by-Step Notes (Click to Expand)

Fractions, Decimals, and Percents are just three ways to show the same value.

Conversion Flowchart

Fraction \(\frac{3}{4}\)
Top รท Bottom
Decimal 0.75
Move decimal 2 right
Percent 75%
Move decimal 2 left

Fraction \(\rightarrow\) Decimal

Divide the numerator by the denominator. (Top number goes "in" the division box)

Example: \(\frac{3}{4} \rightarrow 3 \div 4 = 0.75\)

Decimal \(\rightarrow\) Percent

Move the decimal point TWO places to the RIGHT and add a % sign.

Example: \(0.75 \rightarrow 75\%\)

Percent \(\rightarrow\) Decimal

Move the decimal point TWO places to the LEFT and remove the % sign.

Example: \(62\% \rightarrow 0.62\)

Percent \(\rightarrow\) Fraction

Put the number over 100 and simplify (reduce the fraction).

Example: \(62\% \rightarrow \frac{62}{100} \rightarrow \frac{31}{50}\) (divide top and bottom by 2)

Guided Practice

I Do

Problem: Convert \(\frac{3}{5}\) to a decimal and a percent.

  1. To Decimal: I divide the top by the bottom: \(3 \div 5 = 0.6\).
  2. To Percent: I move the decimal on \(0.6\) two places to the right: \(0.6 \rightarrow 0.60 \rightarrow 60\%\).
  3. Answer: \(0.6\) and \(60\%\).

We Do

Problem: Convert \(8\%\) to a decimal and a fraction.

  1. To Decimal: Move the decimal on \(8\%\) two places to the left. We need to add a zero. \(8.\% \rightarrow 0.08\).
  2. To Fraction: Put \(8\) over \(100\). \(\frac{8}{100}\).
  3. Can we simplify \(\frac{8}{100}\)? (Yes, divide both by 4).
  4. Answer: \(0.08\) and \(\frac{2}{25}\).

You Do

Problem: Convert \(1.2\) to a percent and a fraction.

Percent: \(1.2 \rightarrow 1.20 \rightarrow 120\%\)
Fraction: \(1 \frac{2}{10} \rightarrow 1 \frac{1}{5}\) (or \(\frac{6}{5}\))

(Red) Which is greater, \(\frac{3}{8}\) or \(40\%\)? Show your work.

To compare, make them both decimals.
Convert \(\frac{3}{8}\) to a decimal: \(3 \div 8 = 0.375\).
Convert \(40\%\) to a decimal: \(0.40\).
Since \(0.40 > 0.375\), \(40\%\) is greater.
