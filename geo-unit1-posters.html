Conditional Statement

A logical argument with a hypothesis (p) and a conclusion (q).

If an angle is a right angle, then its measure is 90°.

Symbolically: if p, then q $\quad (\textcolor{#db2777}{p} \rightarrow \textcolor{#2563eb}{q})$

Converse

Formed by interchanging the hypothesis and conclusion.

If an angle measures 90°, then the angle is a right angle.

Symbolically: if q, then p $\quad (\textcolor{#2563eb}{q} \rightarrow \textcolor{#db2777}{p})$

Inverse

Formed by negating the hypothesis and conclusion.

If an angle is not a right angle, then its measure is not 90°.

Symbolically: if not p, then not q $\quad (\sim\textcolor{#db2777}{p} \rightarrow \sim\textcolor{#2563eb}{q})$

Contrapositive

Formed by interchanging and negating the hypothesis and conclusion.

If an angle does not measure 90°, then the angle is not a right angle.

Symbolically: if not q, then not p $\quad (\sim\textcolor{#2563eb}{q} \rightarrow \sim\textcolor{#db2777}{p})$

Deductive Reasoning

Using logic to draw conclusions from definitions, postulates, and theorems.

Statement A: If a quadrilateral has four right angles, then it is a rectangle.

Statement B: Quadrilateral P has four right angles.

Conclusion: Quadrilateral P is a rectangle.

Direct Proof

A logically valid justification based on assumptions, definitions, postulates, and theorems.

Two-Column Proof

Given: $\angle 1 \cong \angle 2$    Prove: $\angle 2 \cong \angle 1$

StatementsReasons
$ \angle 1 \cong \angle 2 $Given
$ m\angle 1 = m\angle 2 $Definition of congruent angles
$ m\angle 2 = m\angle 1 $Symmetric Property of Equality
$ \angle 2 \cong \angle 1 $Definition of congruent angles

Paragraph Proof

It is given that $\angle1\cong\angle2$. By the Definition of congruent angles, $m\angle1=m\angle2$. By the Symmetric Property of Equality, $m\angle2=m\angle1$. By the Definition of congruent angles, $\angle2\cong\angle1$.

Properties of Congruence

Property Segment Congruence Angle Congruence
Reflexive $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{AB}$ $\angle A \cong \angle A$
Symmetric If $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{CD}$, then $\overline{CD} \cong \overline{AB}$. If $\angle A \cong \angle B$, then $\angle B \cong \angle A$.
Transitive If $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{CD}$ and $\overline{CD} \cong \overline{EF}$, then $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{EF}$. If $\angle A \cong \angle B$ and $\angle B \cong \angle C$, then $\angle A \cong \angle C$.

Law of Detachment

If a conditional is true and its hypothesis is true, then its conclusion is true.

If $p \rightarrow q$ is a true conditional statement and $p$ is true, then $q$ is true.

Example

If m∠A > 90°, then ∠A is obtuse.

m∠A = 120°.

Therefore, ∠A is obtuse.

Law of Syllogism

Allows you to draw a conclusion from two conditional statements where the conclusion of one is the hypothesis of the other.

If p $\rightarrow$ q and q $\rightarrow$ r are true, then p $\rightarrow$ r is true.

Example

1. If a rectangle has four congruent sides, then it is a square.

2. If a polygon is a square, then it is a regular polygon.

3. Therefore, if a rectangle has four congruent sides, then it is a regular polygon.

Counterexample

A specific case for which a conjecture is false.

Conjecture: "The product of any two numbers is odd."

Counterexample: $\textcolor{#db2777}{2} \cdot \textcolor{#2563eb}{3} = \textcolor{#16a34a}{6}$

One counterexample proves a conjecture is false.
